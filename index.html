About

One question, followed across disciplines.

I didn't take the obvious route. I trained as a civil engineer, fell for the mathematics underneath it, completed a Master of Philosophy in mathematics, and am now finishing a Master of Science in electrical and computer engineering. From the outside that looks like three careers. From the inside it's one: I kept following a single question across whatever discipline could answer it next.

That question is how agents — cells, people, algorithms — interacting under incentives produce emergent order or collapse into chaos. Rock-paper-scissors dynamics, vaccine hesitancy, traffic crashes, cooperation between AI agents: different costumes, same plot. I love mathematics as a language for this, not just as a tool; the four journal papers I published during my MPhil were the fun part, not the grind.

What I seem to be good at is translating messy, real-world phenomena into precise mathematical structures and then actually solving them — and moving between fields quickly enough to do it where it matters. And it matters more each year. The world has plenty of data scientists; what's scarce are people who can build the theoretical scaffolding that tells us what the data should even mean, in public health, in AI safety, and in security.

My aim is to keep building that scaffolding — to help turn the mathematics of strategic, multi-agent behavior into dependable infrastructure for high-stakes decisions. Right now that means deepening my work on cooperation in multi-agent learning systems, and finding the people and problems worth pointing it at next.

Research / 02

Research

My work sits at the intersection of evolutionary game theory, eco-evolutionary dynamics, and the mathematical modeling of behavior. The common question is how individual choices, made under incentives and uncertainty, add up to collective order or collapse. Rather than list papers, I've organized the work around the questions that drive it.

Theme 01 — cooperation & evolutionary dynamics

Cooperation, cyclic dominance & evolutionary dynamics

When does cooperation survive in a world that rewards defection — and what keeps rock-paper-scissors cycles spinning instead of settling?

Cooperation is fragile: in most strategic settings the rational move is to defect, yet cooperation persists across biology and society. My MPhil work asked when and why. Building on the optional prisoner's dilemma — where players may also abstain — I introduced eco-evolutionary feedback, letting the payoff landscape itself shift with the population's behavior. The result is rich cyclic dominance: rock-paper-scissors-like rotations between cooperate, defect, and abstain that can sustain cooperation indefinitely rather than collapsing to a single equilibrium.

Theme 02 — public health

Behavioral epidemiology & public-health decision-making

Epidemics aren't only biological — they're behavioral. How do testing, treatment, and vaccination choices reshape an outbreak?

Whether people test, isolate, accept treatment, or vaccinate depends on perceived risk, cost, and what their neighbors do. Using game-theoretic models grounded in individual-based risk assessment and replicator dynamics, I've studied how combinations of testing, treatment, and vaccination interact — and which combinations actually drive an outbreak toward control. Alongside the modeling, I worked on field-facing projects on COVID-19 contact-reduction behavior and HPV vaccine hesitancy in Bangladesh.

Theme 03 — AI & security · active frontier

Multi-agent reinforcement learning & security

What happens to cooperation and defection when the agents are learning algorithms instead of people?

This is where I'm heading now. At the University of Florida I've been extending the same questions — when does cooperation emerge, when does it break — to agents that learn. In multi-agent reinforcement learning, strategies aren't fixed; they're discovered through experience, which makes the game-theoretic dynamics both richer and harder to predict. I'm interested in how such systems can be steered toward cooperation and robustness, with cybersecurity as a proving ground where incentives, deception, and defense meet.

Heading toward →  game-theoretic foundations for safer multi-agent AI.

Theme 04 — origins

Modeling real-world risk

Before agents, there were events: can urban traffic crashes be modeled as a self-exciting process that predicts its own aftershocks?

The systems instinct started here. For my civil-engineering thesis I modeled road-traffic crashes in the Dhaka metropolitan area as a self-exciting (Hawkes) point process — treating each crash not as an independent event but as something that raises the short-term probability of further crashes nearby, the way aftershocks follow an earthquake. It was my first taste of finding hidden structure in messy real-world data, and the path from there to game theory has been a straight line in disguise.
